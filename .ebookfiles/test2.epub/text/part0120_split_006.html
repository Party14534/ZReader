Glossary

obesity
Excessive body weight, indicated by a body mass index (BMI) over 30.
food deserts
Geographic areas in which residents do not have easy access to high-quality affordable food. These regions are concentrated in rural areas and poor urban neighborhoods.
body mass index (BMI)
A measure of body fat based on height and weight.
sociology of the body
A field that focuses on how our bodies are affected by social influences. Health and illness, for instance, are determined by social and cultural influences.
sociology of sexuality
A field that explores and debates the importance of biological versus social and cultural influences on human sexual behavior.
health
“A state of complete physical, mental, and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity.”
sick role
A term associated with the functionalist Talcott Parsons to describe the patterns of behavior that a sick person adopts to minimize the disruptive impact of their illness on others.
stigma
Any physical or social characteristic that is labeled by society as undesirable.
epidemiology
The study of the distribution and incidence of disease and illness within a population.
health literacy
One’s capacity to obtain, process, and understand basic health information and services needed to make appropriate health decisions.
heterosexism
The greater status, prestige, and benefits afforded to heterosexual people.
homophobia
An irrational fear of or disdain for gays and lesbians.
socialization of nature
A process by which phenomena that used to be considered “natural,” or given in nature, become social, in that they depend on our personal decisions.
procreative technology
Techniques for influencing the human reproductive process.
Gini coefficient
A standard measure of a country’s economic disparity, where 0 represents perfect equality (everyone has the same income) and 1 represents maximum inequality.
complementary and alternative medicine (CAM)
A diverse set of approaches and therapies for treating illness and promoting well-being that generally fall outside standard medical practices.
biomedical model of health
The set of principles underpinning Western medical systems and practices. The biomedical model of health defines diseases objectively, in accordance with the presence of recognized symptoms, and believes that the healthy body can be restored through scientifically based medical treatment. The human body is likened to a machine that can be returned to working order with the proper repairs.
